The gravel drive narrowed as it left the road, and the sound of the tyres went with it, swallowed between the trees. Elli cut the engine and sat for a moment, hands still on the wheel, listening to the car tick and settle as it cooled. The air beyond the windscreen was cold and unmoving. Old snow lay refrozen in patches over the ground, glazed with frost, catching what little light there was.

The car was a rental from the airport, and it smelled of other people's air freshener. She had driven four hours north with the radio on and then, past the lake, without it, as the road seemed to want. In Korpikylä the lights of Korpi Rauta had been on, yellow behind the paint tins in the window. Ten past four on a Tuesday. Nika would have been behind the counter. Elli had kept her eyes on the junction and driven through.

She stepped out and pulled her jacket tight. The driveway curved away from the car and into the forest, between pine and birch. The trees leaned inward as they always had, tall trunks tilting, their branches vanishing upward into dark. Familiar, but not welcoming. The kind of familiar that made her keep her eyes on the path.

Somewhere past the bend stood the cabin. Hers now, on paper at least, though it had never quite felt that way. She had not planned to come back this year. But the blur on the northern camera, and the silence after it, had left her with little to argue with. The phone in her pocket hunted for reception as she walked, boots loud on frozen gravel, each step carrying her deeper into ten acres of quiet.

The forest gave up the cabin late, a darker shape against darker trees. Elli climbed the last stretch, breath faint in the air, and reached the door. The key was under the north log, wrapped in its square of black plastic. Her fingers knew where to reach.

The door groaned as it opened, the same low, drawn-out sound it had always made. The smell arrived before her eyes had adjusted. Dry pine boards. Dust. A hint of damp needles carried in on her own boots. Beneath it, softer and older, smoke. Not the clean smell of a hearth but something sunk into the grain of the walls over sixty years. The floorboards creaked as she stepped in, too loud in the small space.

The cabin looked unchanged. The square table stood in the centre of the room. The enamel sink, with its hairline crack, caught a sliver of grey light from the small window. The narrow bed against the far wall held its folded blankets, stacked at the foot the way Nika stacked them. And yet the room sat slightly wrong, like a word said too many times. Not broken. Off by a degree.

She set her pack down and stood there, staring at the kitchenette, letting the quiet stretch.

She was nine, maybe ten. Winter. She had woken in the dark to a sound she could not place at first, scraping and rhythmic, something dragged slowly across a floor. She had lain rigid, too afraid to turn her head. Her parents said it was the trees, or the ice settling in the lake. She had accepted that, because the alternative could not be accepted. But the sound had been inside. Under the boards, or in the wall by the bed. She had never told them she knew.

The thought tightened in her chest, and behind it came her grandmother's voice, unconcerned, floury hands shaping karjalanpiirakka at this table.

Of course, it wasn't this cabin back then. My grandparents had the old one, near the slope, before the forest moved.

Before the forest moved. Nobody had asked her what she meant. You didn't ask her things like that. The answers were worse than the remarks.

Elli reached out without looking and flicked the light switch. She knew exactly where it was.

Nothing.

The fusebox was in the porch cupboard, behind the snow shovel. The main breaker had tripped, which meant a surge or a fault or nothing at all. She reset it and the ceiling light came on inside with a weak yellow tremor, and the small sounds of the cabin began: the fridge shuddering awake, a click somewhere in the wall. She stood in the porch a moment longer, one hand on the cupboard door, and then went in and got to work.

Her hands knew the order of things. Wood from the store, kindling split fine, the fire laid and lit. Water from the pump house in two buckets before the light went entirely. The bedding from the chest, shaken out and hung near the hearth to lose its cold. She worked with the slight formality of a woman performing a remembered self, but the work held, as it always held. That was the point of work.

When she went to hang her mug on the hook by the stove, the hook was empty.

She stood looking at it. The blue mug had hung there since before she was tall enough to reach it, handle turned left, a chip in the rim from the summer she and Nika had tried to crack hazelnuts with it. Grandmother's mug, then hers, in the way of everything here. Nika must have moved it. Washed it and put it in the wrong cupboard, or taken it home by mistake among her tools. Elli opened the cupboard above the sink. Plates, the old glasses, the tin of coffee. No mug. She took a white one from her own supplies and let it go.

At dusk she carried a towel and a torch down to the sauna. The stove took the match on the second try and she fed it until the stones began to give back heat, then sat on the top bench in the dark with the door of the firebox glowing at her shins. Steam rose off the stones when she threw water, and the hiss filled the little room and faded. Through the small window the lake lay between the trunks like a dark plate. She had learnt to swim off those rocks. She had held Nika's tenth birthday cake on her knees on this bench because it was the only warm room in October. The part of her that loved this place lived at a certain distance from the rest of her, and in the heat, for a while, the distance closed.

She walked back up with bare feet in her boots, skin steaming, and did not need the torch. That, too, her body remembered.

Dinner was bread and butter and a packet soup, and a glass of wine from the bottle she had bought at the airport. The wine was a New York habit. It had no business here and she drank it anyway, sitting where her grandmother used to sit, and for a while the stillness outside the window read as peace.

Then she took out her phone.

One bar, if she stood at the window and angled the phone towards the road. The voicemail was eleven days old now. She had listened to it so many times that she could have spoken along with it, and she put it to her ear and listened again.

Elli. It's me. You need to come home. Something's wrong with the cabin. I don't know what. Don't go up on your own. Wait. It's... it's lying out there.

The pause before the last line was the worst part. Nika did not pause. Nika said things as she priced hinges, one after another, done. In eleven days Elli had not found a way to hear that pause without her chest going tight.

She had been at her desk when it came in, an ordinary Friday afternoon, and she had stood in the corridor by the lifts and played it twice. Then she had opened a reply and typed, Call me tonight, whatever time, and looked at it, and deleted it, and typed instead, OK. Don't go up again. I'll sort the camera from here. She had sent that one. It had seemed calmer. It had seemed like the version that would not make things worse.

Nika had not answered it.

Four days later the northern camera caught a blur, three seconds of grey smear at the treeline, and went dark, and this time no amount of resetting from the app brought it back. Elli had sat up until two watching the other three feeds, frost and stillness and nothing, and in the morning she had booked the flight. The earliest she could leave was five days out, and she had filled the five days with handovers and told herself they could not be helped. She had not told Nika she was coming. She had told herself there was no point until she knew what she was dealing with, and she had bought food in Rovaniemi so there would be no reason to stop in the village, and she had driven past the lit window of the hardware store at ten past four with her eyes on the junction.

A flight instead of a phone call. It was the kind of answer she was good at.

She opened the folder of saved images next, though she knew them as well as she knew the voicemail. Five frames from the northern camera, from five weeks ago, the second time it died. Frame one: the treeline in washed-out grey, trunks, bracken, frost, and near the left edge something standing where no trunk had been. Frame two, closer. Not much. Enough to see unmarked ground between it and the bracken. Frame three, soft, as if the air in front of the lens had thickened. Frame four.

She had looked at frame four on the subway, in meetings, at three in the morning with the lights of Eighth Avenue on the ceiling. The shape was closer still, tall and narrow and wrong, and the pixels would not resolve it, but that was not the thing. The thing was the trees. The forked birch that should have stood to the right of the frame was left of centre. A spruce with a broken top had moved behind it. Between frames three and four, in under a second of camera time, the forest had rearranged itself like furniture.

Frame five was black.

Could be a deer, she had said to Nika down the phone, five weeks ago. Not a deer, Nika had said, and driven up there. Elli put the phone face down on the table and finished the wine.

The bedroom smelled of the hearth-warmed bedding and, under it, the closed-in cold it had held all year. She lay under the heavy covers with the lamp off and the fire ticking in the other room, and the isolation came on as it always did the first night, not hostile, simply total: the nearest lit window forty minutes south, the phone dead unless she stood by the glass, the dark outside going on and on over the lake and the bog and the low hills with nothing human in it. As a child that had been the best part. Lying in the dark knowing the world had been reduced to one warm room and the people breathing in it.

She thought about the hook by the stove, empty. She thought about the scraping, and her parents' voices through the wall, unbothered.

Tomorrow she would look at the camera in daylight. It was a battery, or moisture in the casing, or a fault in the board. It was a thing with a finite number of causes, and she would work through them in order, because that was what she had flown four thousand miles with a head torch and a multimeter to do.

She slept better than she expected to.

She woke into a silence so complete that for a moment she thought something had happened to her hearing. Then a log shifted in the hearth, a soft collapse of ash, and the room had sound in it again. She lay listening. No wind. No birds. No water. Ten past eight and the window was still black.

By the time the light came up, grey and directionless, she had eaten and dressed and stood twice at the window with her coffee, looking north at the treeline. The young spruce at the edge of the clearing stood white with frost, each needle exact. Nothing had moved out there all morning. Nothing was moving now.

She found the tracks on her second circuit of the cabin, fifty metres out, past the wood store.

A fox, trotting. Small prints, clear pads, an unhurried line crossing the open ground towards the trees. She followed beside it, close but not on it, and then the line stopped. The last print was perfect. Four toes, heel pad, the scrape of a back foot lifting. Beyond it the frost lay clean. No turn. No leap mark. No landing. The animal had crossed forty metres of open ground and then it had simply not continued to exist.

Elli crouched there for a long time with her forearms on her knees.

Your fox learnt to fly, Nika had written, six weeks ago, under a photograph of tracks like these. Elli had looked at the photograph in a taxi on the FDR and put the phone away and answered an email. Standing now where Nika must have stood, in the same flat air, she understood that the photograph had been Nika saying something, in the only register the two of them had left, and that she had not answered it.

She fetched the split log from beside the wall to stand on, and the screwdriver, and the batteries, and made herself busy with the camera, because the tracks were not a job and the camera was.

The casing showed no damage. The battery inside was seated properly and read full on the meter, which made no sense, and the cold of the plastic went through her gloves, and she chose not to think about that, and when she swapped the battery for a new one anyway the green light came on at once, as if the camera had been waiting to be asked in person.

She stood on the log, one hand on the eave, and opened the live feed on her phone. The treeline filled the little screen in colourless grey. Trunks. Bracken. Frost. Her own breath drifted through the bottom of the frame.

She had frame one saved. She held the phone in both hands and flicked between the live image and the old one, back and forth. The bracken matched. The fallen trunk on the right matched. The forked birch did not.

In frame one, the birch stood at the right edge, its double trunk unmistakable. In frame four it had been left of centre. On the live feed it stood left of centre still, and nearer. She flicked back and forth until her thumb ached. There was no version of moisture in a casing that walked a birch tree thirty metres sideways and left it there.

Before the forest moved. Her grandmother had said it as plainly as you would say before the war, a boundary in time that everyone present was assumed to understand, and had gone back to pinching pastry, and was twenty years dead and could not be asked.

Elli climbed down off the log. The sensible actions were known and she listed them to herself while her boots broke the frost crust towards the treeline: photograph everything, drive south until the phone worked properly, call Nika, call someone. The list was still assembling itself when she passed the first trunks. She only wanted to stand at the forked birch. It was a checkable thing. You went to the tree, and you looked at the ground it grew from, and you saw either disturbed earth or undisturbed earth, and then you knew what kind of problem you had. It was two hundred metres. She had a head torch in her pocket, a compass clipped to her jacket, and half a day of light.

The forked birch grew from unbroken ground. Moss on the root flare, decades of needle fall banked against the north side, frost in the bark seams. It had stood there fifty years. It had stood somewhere else five weeks ago. She put her glove against the double trunk and looked back the way she had come and could not see the cabin, which was wrong, because two hundred metres of young spruce should not have closed behind her like that.

It was very quiet in the trees. She noticed it suddenly and all at once, like a noise. A winter forest was quiet, but it was a populated quiet: wings somewhere, snow sliding from a branch, something small bolting before you saw it. This was not that. Nothing moved at any distance in any direction. The forest was still as a room after everyone has left.

Go back, she thought, and walked on.

She was not deciding to. Her feet still had an errand in them and the errand kept extending itself. Another fifty metres, to where the ground began to dip. To the deer path, to see if the deer path was still there. The birches thinned around her and the pines grew older, and the deterioration came on so gradually that she was well inside it before she saw it: needles on the ground grey instead of brown, a dead branch snapping against her sleeve without any spring in it, dry right through, the broken end pale as bone. Whole trees stood dead with their bark on. Nothing had eaten them. Nothing had colonised them. They had just stopped.

The hare was sitting on the path.

In the open, not at the edge, close enough that she should have startled it long before she saw it. It sat with its forepaws together and its ears upright, composed, facing her. It was looking at her the way you look at someone you have been waiting for.

She stopped. It didn't move. She took a step, and another. It sat. She was close enough now to see the frost in its fur, unmelted, and the perfect stillness of its flanks, and that was the detail that reached her: the flanks. A hare's chest fluttered, always, the heartbeat so fast it was a shimmer, even in sleep. There was no shimmer. There was no breath. A hare-shaped thing sat upright at the side of its own tracks, watching her with wet black eyes, and nothing inside it was moving at all.

Elli walked past it. It took everything she had to do it slowly. She did not look back, because the version of the world where it had turned its head to follow her was not one she could afford yet.

The old woods began without a border. The pines gave way to trees she had no name for, or rather to spruce and pine grown so old they had stopped resembling themselves, trunks like standing stones, the canopy knitted shut overhead so that the grey day fell out of the air and something dimmer replaced it. Moss and rot, and under that a sharper smell, mineral, like split stone, like the inside of a rock that has been closed for a long time. The ground was frozen hard, but the cold was not coming down from the air. She could feel it through her boot soles. It was coming up.

She stopped at the deer path, which was not there. No path, no droppings, no browse line. The forest had been emptied, and every animal instinct she had was now saying the same word.

She turned to go back.

The cold came first. Not a deepening chill but a wall of it, laid against her face and the front of her thighs in one moment, as if she had stepped through a doorway into it, except that she had not moved. Her breath stopped showing. The quiet closed over her ears like water.

Something was behind her.

She knew it as skin knows a hand near the face in the dark, with the small hairs, with a sense that has never needed a name. Height. Nearness. A pressure of attention on the back of her neck, patient and entire.

She began not to turn. Then did.

It was there. Close. Close enough that if she had lifted her hand, the hand would have arrived at something. Afterwards she could not assemble it, and stopped trying: fragments were all that had ever been in her eyes. A tallness, leaning very slightly forward, like a thing that had been waiting a long time and did not mind. A suggestion of a face, in the sense that a knot in bark suggests a face, wherever she was not quite looking. The smell of split stone, and under it old smoke. Cold that reached past her skin and her teeth and laid itself along the bone.

What undid her was not the shape of it. It was the attention. It was looking at her the way you look at someone you have been waiting for.

The fear came up through her, and it was not her own. It was too big, too fast, arriving from outside, an instruction rather than a feeling, and it said run, and her body was already obeying before she had finished hearing it. South. Everything in her screamed south.

She ran. Branches took her across the face and shoulders and she went through them, and the cold came with her, pressing at her back, not chasing, present, enormous, a weather front with a mind in it, and she ran until the trees blurred and her breath tore and she never once wondered why the forest tilted around her like water finding a drain.

The pine took her at full speed.

Shoulder, cheekbone, the flat of her forearm, all at once, and her legs were gone before she understood she was falling. The ground came up sideways. Needles against her face. A high clean tone in her left ear that was not a sound, just damage, ringing in the bone.

Everything stopped.

She was on the ground. She knew that. She could not feel the ground. She could see bark very close, and a dark thread of blood crawling into the frost, and past it the forest floor losing its focus. Her chest would not move. The muscles around her ribs had locked shut, and for several seconds the entire world was the effort of pulling air into a body that had forgotten the mechanism.

The breath came at last, thin and wrong, and the pain came on in order: cheekbone, shoulder, then a deep sick flare in her ribs. Blood ran warm from her nose and cooled at her lip.

She could not move. Not would not. The wanting was there and nothing answered it.

Then the stillness beside her changed.

No sound. No shadow. A thickening of the air at her back, low down, near, as of something very large lowering itself with great care to look at what had fallen. The cold deepened until her teeth ached with it. The attention settled over her like snow.

Something at the bottom of her refused. Not bravely. There was no decision in it, no thought at all. It was the reflex that snatches a hand from a stove before the heat has been felt, the animal under all the years of her, the thing that had been alive before she was anyone, and it would not lie still while that leaned over it. Her arm moved. Then the other. The ground swung, her ribs tore at her, and she was on a knee, and then on her feet, swaying, blood on her chin.

She did not look at what was beside her. She did not look.

She went south, bleeding, half-blind, one ear ringing, and the light was already failing between the trunks, going out of the day an hour before it had any right to. She did not count her paces and she did not mark the path. South was the only fact she had left and she spent it all.

The trees thinned. A clearing opened in front of her and she staggered into it without slowing, and looked up, and there, across fifty metres of frost-blackened ground, low roof, dark wall, one window lit warm and yellow, stood the cabin.

She did not stop to question it. She crossed the clearing at a stumbling run and threw herself at the door.

The door gave under her weight and she fell into warmth.

She was across the threshold before any part of her had voted on it, carried by the same terror that had brought her out of the trees, and the door swung shut behind her on its own weight, and the cold was gone. She stood bent over with her hands on her knees, dripping blood onto the floorboards, and the room breathed heat at her from the hearth.

The fire was burning low and steady. Not freshly lit. The logs had collapsed inward and glowed along their centres, hours old, tended. The square table. The enamel sink with its crack. The scorch mark on the hearth stone where a coal had jumped in some winter before her birth. A towel hung warming over the rail by the stove, and on the table, waiting, stood a mug.

None of it was strange to her yet. It was rescue. Inside, said her whole body, not out there, and that was the full extent of the thinking she had in her.

"Christ. What happened to you?"

Nika was at the table. She had been at the table all along, a coffee in front of her, the old green book open under one hand, and she was on her feet before she had finished speaking, a chair scraping back, three steps, and her grip was on Elli's arm. The grip was solid. Warm through the torn sleeve. Too firm to be anything but actual.

"Sit down. Sit. Look at me."

Elli looked at her. Nika, in her work fleece with the Korpi Rauta logo gone bobbled over the chest, hair tied back, four centimetres shorter than her and twice as certain, scowling up at the mess of her face with those pale eyes that had never once in forty years asked permission for anything. The room tilted gently. Nika's other hand came down on her shoulder and steered her into the chair by the fire.

"What did you hit?"

"A tree."

"Running from what?"

Elli didn't answer. Her lungs were still doing the work of three people. Nika looked at her for a moment, then let it go, as she let go of things she meant to come back for, and turned to the stove.

"You called me," she said, with her back turned, lifting the kettle. "So I drove up. Door was open, place was warm, no Elli. Twenty minutes I've been sitting here deciding whether to go out looking and lose the light." She said it without reproach, just facts in a line, hinges priced and done. "Then you come through the door like a shot elk."

I didn't call you. The sentence formed somewhere far back in the fog and did not make it forward. Her head was one enormous pulse. She had run, she had hit a tree. Whole pieces of the afternoon were missing their beginnings. Perhaps she had called from the treeline. Perhaps the phone had found its one bar for her when it mattered. The kettle was already hissing, and the thought sank under the sound and did not come up again.

Nika crouched in front of her with the warmed towel and a bowl of water and cleaned her face, chin steadied between finger and thumb, wiping with short, businesslike strokes that hurt exactly as much as they had to and no more. She looked into one of Elli's eyes and then the other, holding up a finger, follow it, look at me, how many. She pressed along the cheekbone and down the line of her ribs through her shirt, and Elli hissed, and Nika sat back on her heels.

"Nothing's moving that shouldn't. Your head took a knock and your ribs are cracked or bruised, and either way you're not walking anywhere tonight." She said it to the fire, already deciding the evening. "First light, we walk out together. Now drink that."

The mug found its way into Elli's hands. The coffee was pale with milk, no sugar, made in the wide-bottomed pour her grandmother's pot demanded, exactly, precisely how she took it, and she had her lips on the rim before she saw what she was holding.

Blue enamel. The chip in the rim, worn smooth, at the two o'clock of the handle. She turned it slightly and her thumb went to the chip on its own, as it had gone there through every summer of her childhood.

Something happened in her chest that she had no room for. The hook by the stove had been empty last night. The thought lifted its head somewhere in the fog. But the mug was here, so the mug had been somewhere, in a cupboard, in Nika's bag, wherever things went, and the coffee was warm and made for her, made the right way, without asking, because Nika had never had to ask, and the first mouthful was the exact taste of being twelve years old with lake water in her hair. Her eyes stung. She kept them down until it passed.

This was what she had walked away from. Someone who knew the chip in the rim, which side she bruised easiest, how much silence she needed before she could talk. Twenty years of texts about batteries, and it had been here the whole time, a day's travel and a straight road.

"Eat first," Nika said, "talk after," and put a pan on the stove.

She could not have said when the first wrong thing happened, because at the time it was only almost.

She was watching the window while Nika cooked. Frost was building on the inside corners of the glass, feathering as frost does, and she followed the pattern idly with her eyes and found that it was not feathering. The lines were branching from a centre, concentric, grained, the pattern of growth rings in a split branch, of wood laid open. She blinked, and it was frost, doing what frost does, and the pan spat behind her and Nika said something about the state of the woodpile, and the window was just a window with weather on it.

Her head had hit a tree. That was the file it went into. The file had room for more.

They ate at the table, tins made into something better than she would have made of them, and Nika talked as she had always talked, in short runs with work in them. Jukka's knee. A winter of burst pipes. The tourist who had come in asking for a bear bell in November. It was easy. It was so easy that Elli's throat kept closing around the food. On the plane she had rehearsed an apology, the shape of one, in case it came to actual talking, and none of it was needed. Something in that snagged, low down, and she was too tired to reach after it.

She let the evening stay easy.

Nika reached across to take her plate, stacking it one-handed, and Elli looked at the hand on the plate and the hand was wrong. The knuckles stood too proud, too dark, the skin over them pulled dry and ridged, and for the space of a breath the ridges had grain in them, the fine long grain of weathered birch, knots where the joints should be. Then Nika shifted her grip, and it was a hand, chapped from a life of winter hardware, familiar down to the white scar at the base of the thumb where the box cutter had slipped the year they were seventeen.

"You're staring," Nika said, amused.

"Sorry. Concussion."

"Mm." She carried the plates to the sink. "You always did stare. You used to stare at things until they confessed."

Elli laughed, and laughing hurt her ribs, and let the hand be a hand. The fire moved. The room was warm. Outside, dark had come down over the clearing like a lid over a pot.

It was Elli who opened the door. She had meant only to look for her car, some part of her still keeping accounts: the rental at the end of the drive, Nika's Toyota beside it, the ordinary arithmetic of vehicles. She lifted the latch and stood in the doorway with the warmth pressing at her back, and the arithmetic died.

There was no drive. There was no car, hers or Nika's. The clearing ran fifty metres to a treeline that was not the treeline, trees too old and too dark and grown too close together, interlocked overhead where they should have opened to the sky, and the ground was not frost-white but a deep matt black, as if burnt. Above it all the night was wrong. No stars, and no cloud to blame for it. A flat black ceiling that gave the impression, without any feature she could point to, of not being far away.

The cold reached in past her and stirred the fire behind.

"First light," Nika said, from close behind her shoulder. There was no alarm in her voice at all. "We'll walk out at first light, together, on the compass. No sense in it now, in the dark, with your head." A hand settled on Elli's shoulder, warm, certain. "Come inside. I'm here now."

It was the right thing to say. It was word for word what the real Nika would have said, standing in a wrong doorway looking at a wrong world, because Nika's answer to fear had always been to reduce it to a task with a time attached. The words were perfect. Everything was perfect, and fifty metres away the black ground waited with the woods behind it, and Elli was injured and exhausted and twenty years starved of exactly this voice saying exactly these things.

She stepped back from the doorway. She let the door close. She chose the warm room.

"We should get some sleep if we're walking out early," Nika said, later. "Sauna will have to wait. You'd cook your brain in that state anyway."

She said it exactly as she stacked the fire for the night, not looking, and then she pulled the spare mattress from the chest, the one that had lived there since before either of them could carry it, and laid it out on the floor by the narrow bed, and shook a blanket over it, and the whole arrangement assembled itself out of forty summers of habit. You take the bed, I'm nearer the fire. The room like a tent around the two of them. It had been the geography of every childhood night here: Elli against the wall, Nika between her and the door, because Nika was between everyone and the door, it was where she lived.

"Like when we were kids," Nika said, and turned down the lamp.

Elli lay under the heavy covers with her ribs aching in their slow tide and watched the firelight move on the boards of the ceiling. The wind did not blow. The trees did not creak. Somewhere far below the floor, the cold pressed upward against the warm room like a palm laid flat against timber, and the room held.

"You remember running to the lake," Nika said in the dark. Not a question. Her voice had gone low and even, the voice for after lights-out. "You'd go in front, with the towel round your neck. Every time. And you never once looked back to see if I was coming." A log settled. "You knew I'd be there. That was the thing. You never had to look, your whole life, because I was always going to be there."

The fire ticked. Elli lay very still.

It was a true memory, and it was hers as much as Nika's, the pale path, the fish-smell of the water at dusk, the towel. But it was not a thing Nika would say. It was a thing Nika would die before saying. She had watched Nika fail to say things her whole life. It was one of the loves between them, the not-saying, the words put down and carried instead. What she had just heard was the inside of Nika, the tender counted grief of her, spoken aloud in Nika's easy voice as if it cost nothing, and it had been offered to her like the coffee: exactly what she wanted, made without asking.

"Night, Elli," Nika said.

"Night."

She lay and listened to Nika breathing in the dark below her, the long even breaths of someone going down into sleep, and the breaths were the last thing, the thing that finished it, because they did not change. Sleep had weather in it. Breath should have gone ragged at its edge, caught, slowed, shifted with the body shifting. This breathing was a tide without a moon, in and out, identical, patient, and she lay in the warmth with her eyes open and counted forty breaths and every one of them was the same breath.

The hare, sitting composed at the side of the path, its flanks not moving at all.

Elli kept her own breath slow. In the file that her concussion had been keeping, the papers began, quietly, to line themselves up. The frost. The knuckles. The smile that had come a half-beat late. The mug, whole in her hands tonight, and the hook that had been empty last night. You called me, and she had not called anyone. She reached back through the fog of the afternoon now, deliberately, and there was no calling in it anywhere, only running.

Her phone was in her jacket on the peg by the door. She lay a long while before she eased out from under the covers and got it, one held breath at a time, and the screen would not wake. Not flat-battery dark. Dark all through, like the sky over the clearing. And dinner, late: tins she had never bought, from a cupboard that held no wine, though her own bottle stood corked on a counter somewhere south of this room.

And beneath all of it, the flaw so wide she had been living inside it all evening: it didn't hurt. Twenty years, and none of it had been in the room. Nika had crossed to her and gripped her arm in the first second, without a flicker, without the doorway pause of two people who used to be everything and now shared a key under a log. The real Nika would have come. She knew that with a certainty that ached worse than the ribs. The real Nika would have dropped everything and come, and there would have been a beat, at the threshold, with her boots half unlaced. There would have been the light awkward hell of it, eyes not quite landing, both of them deciding how to stand. The distance was real. She had made it herself, message by unsent message, year by year, and it could not be waved off, and the thing breathing tidally in the dark below her had waved it off. It knew the years. It had counted them out of Nika somewhere in those woods, along with the towel and the mug and the lake path. What it did not have was the room: how the two of them would actually stand in one, after all this time. Nobody had ever seen that. It had never happened anywhere. She had made sure it never happened.

The fire had burned down while she thought, further than it should have, and the warmth had pulled back from the walls towards the hearth, and along the floor, where the light was lowest, the boards had gone the deep matt black of the ground outside. When she looked directly, they were boards. The room held its shape the way the smile had held its face: from attention. From hers.

Elli lay in the dark of the wrong cabin, beside the thing wearing her oldest friend, and let the knowing finish.

She did not sleep, and she did not pretend to think about what to do, because there was no list of options to work through and for once in her life she did not reach for one. What there was, in the last hours of that night, was an accounting.

She lay in the warmth it kept for her and paid attention to what she had done. She had drunk the coffee. She had let herself be cleaned and settled and decided over. She had heard her childhood handed back to her across a dark room and she had said night in return, and lain in the bed it made, in the room it built, hour after hour, wanting it. That was the part there would be no giving back. It had not taken anything from her. She had sat at its table and handed everything across, to a friend.

Grey came into the window at last, the wrong grey, sourceless. Below her, the breathing stopped, without any of the business of waking, and she heard the thing that was not Nika get up in one motion and set the kettle on.

Elli rose. Her ribs had stiffened in the night and the room swung once and settled. She dressed slowly, with her back to the stove, and when she turned, the thing stood in Nika's fleece in the lamplight, pouring coffee into the blue mug, and it held the mug out to her, and its face made Nika's morning face, the half-scowl before the day's first words.

"Drink up. We'll want the light."

"No," Elli said. "Thank you."

Not sharp. Nothing that could be argued with. She heard her own voice come out in the register she used across desks in glass rooms four thousand miles from here, level, courteous, spaced. The register of a woman speaking to someone she does not know well.

The mug stayed in the air between them.

"You'll want something in you," it said. Nika's cadence, exact. "It's a long walk on the compass."

"It was good of you to drive up," Elli said. "With the roads like this. I know it's a long way to come for someone else's problem."

Something moved behind the face. Not on it. Behind it. The warmth of the expression stayed where it had been put, and underneath, something adjusted, tried a purchase, found none.

"Elli." Reproachful, warm, a hand extended to the old shorthand. "It's me."

"We haven't spoken properly in four years," Elli said, in the same level voice. "I missed your mother's funeral. I sent flowers from an app. When you left me the message I listened to it eleven times and I wrote back about the camera." She kept her eyes on its eyes. That was the hardest part. The eyes were so exactly right. "The last time I was in that shop you'd taped a photograph of me to the monitor by the till. Brown shoulder, sun in my hair. Fourteen years old, that picture. You'd have taken it down if you'd known I'd seen it, so I never said. That is what we are now. She doesn't look at me the way you looked at me last night. Nobody has looked at me like that for twenty years. I made sure of it."

The kettle went on hissing on the stove, and then it did not, though no one had moved it.

The thing wearing Nika set the mug down on the table without a sound. When it spoke again the voice was still Nika's voice, and the warmth was gone from under the words, as heat goes out of a stone.

"She counted the years," it said. "Fourteen since you slept a night here. She has a number for it. She never once asked you to come home. Twenty years, and the message was the first time, and she sat at her kitchen table with the frost coming up the glass and made herself say it. And you wrote back about a camera."

Elli stood very still. True things. All of them true, and Nika's, taken from her along with the towel and the lake path, grief that had been counted out in private and was now being spent back at her, flat, exact, in Nika's own voice in a warm room. Lies she could have walked away from.

"I know," she said. It came out quieter than she meant, and she straightened her back and finished it, because there was one thing left in the account and it was Nika's too, and it belonged here. "She told me one more thing. On the message. She said, it's lying out there."

The room went still. Even the fire.

"She warned me," Elli said. "I came anyway. I stayed the night. That part is mine." She took the breath her ribs allowed. "And you are still not her."

The pretence stopped.

It did not fall away, or melt, or turn. It stopped, mid-stance, between one breath and none, as an actor stops when the take is over: the face still made of Nika's face but no longer being worn from the inside, the eyes still pale and still open and no longer looking at her, or at anything. The lamp burned. The fire had gone to a grey that gave no light. Around the edges of the room, without her watching it happen, the black of the ground outside had come up the walls to the height of the window sills, and the frost on the glass had finished its pattern, rings within rings, the grain of a thing split open. The warmth remaining in the room ended in a clean line half a metre from the hearth. Nothing in the cabin was interested in her any more. She could feel the withdrawal of the attention like a pressure lifted off her skin, all at once, everywhere: the sensation of a book being closed on a page.

Elli took her jacket off the peg. Her hands did the zip, the buttons at the collar, working on their own, well made, well trained. She put on her boots at the door, and stood, and did not look at what was standing by the stove in Nika's fleece. Whatever was under the face had never once been shown to her, in the woods, on the ground, in this room. She kept it that way, and lifted the latch, and walked out.

The cold met her at the threshold. She went across the black ground towards the treeline with her ribs in one hand and the head torch on, and the woods took her in without any interest at all. No path offered itself. No clearing opened. The trees stood where trees stand, and she walked between them in the dark of the morning, one tree and then the next, and nothing arranged itself, and nothing followed, and that was the worst hour of it, worse than the running: to move through that forest mattering to nothing, a small warm error the woods had finished with, walking south on the little compass clipped to her jacket because the compass was the only thing out here she knew to be real.

Twice she went down. Once on ice hidden under the crust, once because her legs simply stopped, and the second time she lay against the frozen ground until her ribs agreed to lift her again.

The frost, when it came back to the ground, came back patchy and real, grey-white, catching the torch. The trees thinned into birch. Somewhere off to her left a mass of snow slid from a branch and landed, a soft ordinary crash, the first sound the world had made in hours, and she stood still with her eyes shut and listened to the last of it like music.

The light came up while she walked, real light with a direction to it. She crossed her own tracks from the morning before, boot prints in frost with a night's new crystal grown over them, coming out of the trees fifty metres from the wood store, and beyond them, low roof, dark wall, dead windows, no smoke, stood the cabin.

Elli walked past the fox tracks without looking at them and let herself in with the key from her pocket.

Inside was cold and dark and smelled of yesterday's fire. Through the bedroom door the bed stood open where she had left it. The wine bottle stood corked on the counter, the empty glass beside it. By the stove, the hook was empty.

She stood in front of it for a while, in her jacket, in the cold. Then she went to the window with the phone and held it to the glass until the single bar surfaced, and rang Nika.

It rang four times. Long enough for her to see herself in the dark of the enamel sink, one cheekbone swollen, one eye going black.

"Elli."

And there it was. The pause. Nika said her name and then there was a silence with twenty years in it, the sound of her oldest friend not knowing how to speak to her, boots half unlaced in a doorway, both of them deciding how to stand, and Elli's eyes went hot with a relief she could never have explained to anyone. The damage was in the line, real and hers. It was the most human sound she had ever heard.

"You went up," Nika said.

"Yes."

"Alone." A breath went out through the word. "I told you to wait."

"I know."

The line hummed with the distance, satellites and weather. When Nika spoke again her voice had gone quieter, the pricing-gun flatness with something under it that neither of them was going to name today.

"There's coffee at the shop," she said. "Come down before the light goes. Drive slow past the lake."

"I'm coming down," Elli said. "Niks."

The pause this time was shorter. "Drive slow," Nika said, and rang off.

Elli stood at the window a moment longer. Outside, the clearing lay white and still under the first proper daylight, her own tracks and the fox's written across it, and the treeline stood at the distance it stood at today.

She was packing when the scraping began.

It came from below, under the boards, or along them, slow and rhythmic, something dragged with patience across a floor, the same sound out of the same dark she had lain rigid in at nine years old while her parents' voices explained it away through the wall. She was not nine now. She knew what she had been listening to all her life. Not something trying to get in. Something letting her know it was there.

It had moved her once, and she had run, and the running had taken her exactly where it wanted her. Elli set the bag down. She pulled out the chair, her grandmother's chair, and sat at the table in her jacket with her hands flat on the wood, facing the empty hook, and listened.

The scraping went on for a while. Then it stopped.

She waited.
