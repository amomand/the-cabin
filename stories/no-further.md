By four o'clock the mats inside Korpi Rauta had gone grey with meltwater. Nika stood behind the counter with a pricing gun in one hand and a strip of brass hinges in the other, listening to the old heater tick above the door. Every time it came on, the smell of dust moved through the shop, dry and warm over the sharper smells of paint, rubber boots, engine oil.

Her phone buzzed under the counter. She left it there until she had finished labelling the hinges. Twelve pieces. Two missing screws in the bottom pack. She wrote that on a slip for Jukka, then reached down and turned the phone over.

*North camera again. Could be a fox. Any chance you can check when you're free? Key's in the same place.*

Elli. Her eyes flicked to the small photograph taped to her monitor, years old, cropped from something larger. Brown shoulder. Sun behind her hair. Nika had meant to change it and never had.

She read the message twice. Outside, a van went by slowly, tyres hissing through half-frozen slush. The shop window showed her own face behind the reflection of paint tins.

*Today?* she wrote. Three dots appeared, disappeared, appeared again.

*Only if easy. I'm getting alerts and then nothing. Other feeds are fine.*

Nika looked at the dark beyond the window. Too early for the shortcut across the lake to hold, too cold for the forest track to be clear. The drive to the cabin would take forty minutes, maybe longer. She had not been there since March, when the last snow still held in the tree shade and she had replaced the loose latch on the wood store because Elli had noticed it on a camera feed from New York.

*I'll go after close,* she wrote. The answer came quickly. *Thank you. Key should still be under the north log.*

It would be. Nika had put it back there herself in March. She started to count the years since Elli had been back there and stopped. The cabin was the only thing Elli still messaged her about.

Nika put the phone face down. The pricing gun had left a red square on her thumb where the handle pressed. She rubbed it once and picked up the next packet.

At five, Jukka came out of the back with his coat already on.

"You locking?"

"I'll do it."

He looked towards the window, then at her. "Roads are bad."

"Roads are always bad." She stopped. "I'm heading up by the lake."

"Lake road?"

She put the cash drawer into the safe. "Elli's camera is acting up."

Jukka zipped his coat. His fingers were thick and cracked at the knuckles. "Tomorrow would be better. Or maybe not at all."

"Maybe."

He made a sound through his nose and left. The bell over the door rang once. The shop held the little cold he had let in. Nika stood in it a moment before turning off the lights.

The road ran beside the lake for six kilometres before it narrowed into forest. At first there were houses, low squares of yellow window light behind spruce, a tractor parked under a blue tarpaulin, smoke laid flat above a sauna chimney. Then the houses thinned. The lake opened on her left, dull pewter under a white sky, its edge already skinned with ice among the reeds.

Nika drove with both hands on the wheel. She had learnt the road at seventeen, in her father's old Volvo, when he still believed a person should know how to steer out of a slide before they were allowed to drive anywhere useful. The bends were in her hands more than in her eyes. Tight left after the broken culvert. Long rise before the birches. Slow at the rock where elk crossed.

Past the lake, the radio began to lose itself. A man's voice softened into static, returned for two words, then went thin and vanished. Nika turned it off. The cabin road did not need music.

The last stretch was gravel under a hard skim of ice. Pine pressed close on both sides. Their branches hung low, heavy with frost that had not yet become snow. No twig ticked in the cold. The sound of the tyres stayed under the car.

When the cabin appeared, it did so all at once. Low roof, dark wall, the small window catching a square of dead light. It had always looked smaller after the first glimpse, but the first glimpse still held the shape of childhood. A place beyond school and town and winter roads. Bare legs on the path to the lake. Elli running ahead with a towel around her neck, not looking back because she knew Nika was there.

Nika parked beside the wood store and cut the engine. The car settled around her. The heater fan clicked twice and stopped. She sat with one hand still on the key. The air beyond the windscreen had no weather in it. Nothing moved in it and nothing was about to.

She opened the door. Cold came in hard at knee height. It found the gap above her boots and the inside of her wrists. Her breath showed white, then broke apart in front of her face and hung lower than it should have. She got out, shut the door, and listened.

There was no wind in the trees. No water under the ice. No branch settling. The silence had grain. She could feel it against the skin inside her ears.

"Fox," she said. The word went nowhere.

The key was under the north log, wrapped in the same square of black plastic. Her fingers knew where to reach. The lock resisted, then turned with the small rough scrape she remembered.

Inside, the smell arrived before the dark had separated into objects. Dry pine boards. Dust. Old wool. The faint smoke that never quite left the walls. Under it sat the closed-in smell of a place that had not been warmed for months.

Nika found the switch by touch. The ceiling light came on with a weak yellow tremor. Everything looked fine. The table stood in the centre of the room. The sink was clean. The narrow bed against the far wall had its folded blankets stacked at the foot. Elli's old blue mug still hung from the hook by the stove, handle turned left, a chip in the rim from the summer they had tried to crack hazelnuts with it. The room looked empty in the usual way.

Nika took one step in and stopped. The chairs were around the table. Four chairs, one to each side, each set at the same distance from the table edge. Each angled inward by the same small degree. Their front legs made a square on the floorboards. Someone had measured them with a ruler and then taken the ruler away.

She kept her gloves on. "Elli," she said, though Elli was not there, "you need a hobby." The words sounded thin. She crossed to the hearth.

The grate was empty. Not banked, not cold with old ash, empty. The brick at the back showed black where smoke had marked it, but the iron tray below had been cleaned to dull metal. She crouched and looked beneath the grate. Nothing. No grey crust in the corners. No flecks where the brush had missed.

She had left ash here in March. There had been no reason not to.

She touched the tray with one gloved finger. The metal was cold, ordinary cold.

On the shelf above the bed, a book stood spine-out from the others by the width of a thumb. Green cloth cover, title gone pale. Nika looked at it long enough to know she did not remember seeing it like that. She did not go over.

The northern camera was mounted under the eave, aimed at the treeline. Outside, the light had thinned while she was inside. Nika took the small screwdriver and spare batteries from her coat pocket and stood on the split log by the wall to reach it.

The casing showed no mark. She opened it carefully. The battery inside was seated properly.

When she touched the casing with the back of her fingers, the cold passed through the glove.

She pulled her hand away. The plastic should have held the air temperature, no more. This was colder than the air. It did not sting at the surface. The cold went past the surface and reached the bones.

The new battery made no difference. No green light. No red.

She stood on the log and looked past the dead camera.

The trees north of the cabin were close. They had always been close. The old clearing narrowed there, where young spruce had taken back the edge. Between the trunks, the ground fell slightly towards the lake, though from here the lake could not be seen. Frost had gathered on the bracken. Each frond was white and exact.

Nika closed the casing and climbed down. The other three cameras worked. She checked them one by one, rubbing warmth into her right hand between each. East, south, west. Green light. Green light. Green light.

By the time she came back inside, the room had darkened at the corners. Forty minutes home. Forty minutes back in the morning. The roads hardening as the temperature dropped. Nika looked at the empty hearth, then at the stacked birch beside it. "Fine."

The fire took on the third match. At first the kindling smoked and spat, then the birch caught properly and the hearth began to give off a workable heat. The room changed by degrees. Metal expanded with small ticks. Damp left the stones around the fire. The smell of old wool loosened from the blankets.

Nika ate rye bread and cheese standing by the counter. She put water on the stove for coffee she did not need. Her phone had one bar near the window if she held it up and angled it towards the road.

*North camera dead. No damage. Others fine. Staying tonight, will reset again tomorrow.*

Elli answered after ten minutes. *You really don't have to stay.*

Nika watched the message sit on the screen. A bubble of melted snow slid down the outside of the glass and stopped halfway.

*Too late. Fire's on.*

*Thank you, Niks.*

No one called her that now. Not even Elli, except sometimes when the past slipped its hand into a message before either of them could stop it. Nika set the phone on the sill and did not answer.

The cabin had its night sounds once the fire was established. Wood settling. Heat touching nails. A faint suck in the chimney when the wind shifted. Normal sounds. She knew them well enough to sleep under them.

She set one chair facing the hearth and left the other three at the table. The neatness of their arrangement annoyed her more when she could not see it, so she disturbed it. The legs scraped over the boards with a plain, healthy noise.

Nika lay down on the narrow bed in her thermal layers and kept her socks on. Smoke moved softly in the walls. Her last clear thought was that she should have brought coffee from home instead of whatever Elli kept in the tin.

In the dream, she stood in the cabin doorway.

The door was open. Beyond it, the forest had no depth. Every branch, every needle, every frost-white stem lay flat and sharp, painted against something Nika could not see through. The path to the lake was there. The split log was there. The dead camera was there above her left shoulder, though she did not turn to look.

There was no sound. Nika opened her mouth. Her jaw moved. Her throat worked. Nothing left her. The silence was not outside her. It filled the space behind her tongue and the hollows beside her teeth.

She knew she should not step out. She could not remember who had told her.

*

Morning came grey and windless. Nika woke with her hand closed around the blanket edge. The fire had gone low but not out. A red seam held under the ash. For a few seconds she did not move. The room smelled of smoke, wool, and the sour metal of cooled coffee.

The three chairs stood where she had left them at the table. The fourth faced the hearth. She dressed, fed the fire once, and took coffee outside in Elli's chipped blue mug.

The air had thickened overnight. Breath did not lift. It spread and stayed near her face. The clearing looked untouched, each patch of frost intact except for her own boot marks between cabin and wood store. She found the fox tracks fifty metres north.

They began near the low bracken, a neat trotting line crossing the open ground towards the trees. Small prints. Clear pads. The animal had moved without hurry. Nika followed them with the mug in one hand, careful not to step too close.

At first she thought the ground had changed. Rock under the frost, perhaps, or a bare patch where prints would not hold. She crouched.

The last print was perfect. Four toes. Heel pad. A scrape where the back foot had lifted. Beyond it, frost lay clean and unbroken. Not wind-smoothed. Not filled in. Clean.

No turn. No leap mark. No scuff. No second line returning to the trees. Nika took a photograph. Her knees cracked when she stood.

The coffee in the mug had gone cold. She tipped it onto the ground beside her boot and watched the dark splash soak in. Under her breastbone, something pulled once. Not pain. Weight. A small stone swallowed whole.

She sent the photograph to Elli when she got back to the cabin. *Your fox learnt to fly.*

This time, when she put the second new battery into the northern camera, the green light came on at once.

Nika kept her fingers away from the casing after that. She locked the cabin, checked the door twice, and drove home with the heater high enough to dry her eyes.

On Friday evening, the bar at Korpikylä smelled of wet wool and frying onions. The television over the counter showed a ski race with the sound off. Seven people sat where they usually sat, which made eight when Nika came in. She took the stool beside Matti because the other end of the bar was under the speaker and someone had put on old tango music.

Matti was talking before she had both gloves off. "Not taking the lake road after dark," he said to Pekka, who nodded with the grave attention he gave to weather and tax notices.

"You don't take it after dark because you drive like a priest," Pekka said.

"Priests get where they're going."

The woman behind the bar set a beer in front of Nika without asking. Nika wrapped both hands around the glass for the cold of it. "Elk?" she asked.

Matti looked at her. He was in his seventies, narrow through the shoulders now, with a white scar under one eye from a chainsaw chain that had snapped before Nika was born.

"What?"

"On the lake road."

"No."

He lifted his glass, did not drink. "Still days."

The old words came back with the taste of well water. Her grandmother had used them rarely, never with weight on them. A phrase dropped near a window. A reason to bring washing in. Days when sound carried wrong. Days when old people did not go out and children were told not to make work.

"No reason to."

"Is there a reason now?"

"You said that."

"Then don't make me say it twice."

Pekka reached for the bowl of peanuts and pushed it between them without looking up.

A man two stools down said the word into his glass. Hiisi.

The man drank without answering.

Nika drank half her beer. The old words stayed in her mouth after the beer had gone down.

Elli called three days later while Nika was unloading snow shovels from a pallet at work. The phone vibrated in her back pocket, stopped, then started again. Elli did not usually call twice.

Nika let the shovel handles rest against her shoulder and answered. "What?"

"Are you somewhere you can look at something?"

The line had that thin overseas delay. Elli sounded awake in the wrong part of her day. "I'm at work."

"I got footage. From the north camera."

"Send it."

The images arrived one by one. Frame one showed the northern treeline in washed-out grey. Trunks, bracken, frost. Near the left edge, something stood where no trunk had been.

She went a day later than promised. The delay was easy to defend. A delivery came in wrong. A hinge order had to be corrected. Rain fell at noon and froze by three, and no one with sense drove the lake road when fresh ice sat over old ruts.

By the next morning the excuses had hardened into something she could stand on. Jukka had told her to take what time she needed. He had not asked how much. Nika packed two torches, a thermal camera from the store, spare batteries, a pry bar, and a coil of inspection cable. She did not pack an overnight bag. Then she went back inside and packed one. In the kitchen she filled a box with bread, tins, butter, and her own coffee. Enough for days. She was not staying days.

The road felt longer.

There was no obvious change. The lake still lay to her left. The broken culvert still took the car hard through the suspension. The birches still leaned away from the rise where wind had shaped them for years. Each bend gave up what came after it later than she remembered. The trees met above the road in places she did not remember. Branches reached low enough that frost scraped the roof once, a dry whisper over metal.

The radio stayed off. At the clearing, Nika parked in the same place as before and left the engine running.

The cabin sat behind the windscreen, low and dark. No smoke. No movement at the window. The northern camera showed a small black bead under the eave. She could see the green light from the car if she leaned forward and narrowed her eyes.

The clock on the dash changed from 11:36 to 11:37. Heat blew over her boots. The engine made its idle tremor. Her left hand rested on the door handle without pressing it. At 11:38, frost slid from a pine branch and fell in a loose white sheet. It made no sound she could hear. At 11:39, she turned off the engine. The quiet came up against the glass.

Inside, the first breath hurt the soft part of her nose. The cabin was colder than outside. Not at the door, where cold might have gathered. In the centre of the room. It sat there, away from the walls, away from the window, dense enough that she slowed before entering it.

The chairs faced her. All four of them. Their backs were to the table, the front legs aligned along a line Nika could not stop seeing. They were evenly spaced, each turned towards the door. Waiting was not the right word. The right word would have made them easier to move.

Nika stood with the overnight bag still on her shoulder. The green book lay open face-down on the shelf. Its spine had been cracked flat. She could see a pale fan of pages on either side.

She put the bag down beside the door and went to the hearth.

The fire took badly. Birch that should have been dry hissed under the match. Smoke pressed into the room before the chimney found its pull. Nika stayed crouched, feeding it thin pieces, then thicker ones, until flame held.

Warmth came from the hearth and stopped. At first she thought it was only the draught. She stood and held out her hand. Warmth covered her fingers. Her wrist stayed cold. She moved her hand forward and back. Fingers warm. Wrist cold. A clean border lay in the air about half a metre from the hearthstone.

She took the thermal camera from her bag. On the screen the hearth showed white, the boards in front of it orange going yellow. Then blue. No band between. Heat faded by degrees. That was what heat did. She followed the border with the lens. Where it crossed the birch stack, the same log read warm at one end and cold at the other, and the change was one pixel wide. She set the camera on the table, screen down.

She stepped over the border. Cold closed around her knees.

For a while she did nothing. The fire worked behind her, making its small honest sounds. The room did not take what it offered.

Nika turned the chairs one by one to face the hearth. The first scraped loudly. The second made almost no sound. She stopped with her hand on its back, then pulled harder. The legs grated over the boards.

The book stayed open on the shelf. She went to it. The pages smelled dry, without dust. She closed the cover without looking down.

The windows were sealed. The door sat square in its frame. No boards had lifted. No pipe had burst below the sink. The electrical panel gave no warning smell, no scorch mark, no loose wire visible under the torch. Nika checked because checking was work.

Outside, the north wall sat in shade though the rest of the clearing held the colourless noon. She unlatched the crawl space cover with the pry bar. Frozen soil flaked away from the frame. The gap under the cabin breathed out no warmer air. She lay on her stomach and pushed the torch in first.

Earth. Joists. Old cobwebs. The underside of the floorboards, rough-sawn and pale where no smoke had ever touched them. She pulled herself in on elbows and knees. Her coat caught twice on splintered wood. The space narrowed near the north wall, where the ground rose slightly under the cabin.

The torch beam crossed a dark patch of earth. It was no wider than a doormat. The soil around it was hard with normal frost, grey-brown and granular. The patch itself was almost black. Smooth. It had no crystals on it. No crumbs. No small roots showing in the beam.

Nika took off one glove with her teeth and pressed her palm flat to it. The cold entered at once. It was not surface cold. It began below the skin. Her fingers numbed before her palm did. She pressed harder.

The earth held. Then the holding changed.

No movement showed under her hand. Nothing rose. Still, pressure met pressure somewhere below the place she touched. Nika stopped breathing.

The torch lay against her wrist. Its light shone over the earth, the joist, the cobwebs grey with dust. Her breath should have clouded in front of her. It did not.

She backed out slowly. At the opening, her boot slipped on frozen grass and the back of her head struck the frame. The sound was small, almost polite.

She sat outside with mud on her sleeves, one glove missing, and held her bare hand under her arm until feeling returned in bright, needling bursts.

By then the light was going. Forty minutes of ice in the dark, or a fire already lit. She brought in more wood.

The second night, she dropped Elli's blue mug while she was drying it. The crash folded inward before it reached the walls. The pieces came to rest around her boots with tiny clicks.

She crouched to pick them up. A crescent of blue enamel sat under the table. She reached for it and saw the chairs.

They had moved. Not far. The front legs were no longer square to the hearth.

Nika swept the floor and left the chairs as they were.

On the third morning, the green book was closed.

She had left it open the previous night, face-up on the table with a tin of screws holding down one page. Not to read it. To know.

Now it stood on the shelf, spine-out, the titleless green cloth flush with the others.

Nika looked at the screws on the table. They remained arranged in the rough line she had poured from the tin. One lay apart, near the edge, its sharp tip pointing at the door.

"Boiled water," she said. Her voice sounded ordinary enough. She filled the kettle. "Checked west camera. Checked south camera. Checked east." The kettle went on the stove. "North camera still green. No damage on casing." She opened the tin of coffee and measured too much into the press. "Floorboards sound. Door sound. Windows sound."

She stopped. No sound answered. Nika closed the coffee tin.

Two nights, and she had chosen neither of them. Each evening the drive home came apart quietly: ice on the road, wood already in, the light gone. The box from home was emptying a day at a time. On the fourth night, she woke to pressure at the wall.

No knock. Nothing as clear as that. A faint force at the edge of hearing, like a palm laid flat against timber and held there.

Nika lay on the narrow bed with her eyes open. The fire had gone to embers. Her right hand had gone numb again though it was under the blanket with the rest of her.

The pressure did not repeat. It did not need to.

At 02:14, by the light of her phone, the chairs faced the hearth. At 03:03, they still faced it. At 03:51, a coal fell in the grate and sent up three brief sparks.

When morning came, the silence had edges. She felt them when she crossed the room. A thickening near the table. A softer place by the hearth where the fire had warmed the stones but not the air beyond them. A dense corner near the shelf where the green book stood.

The hearth was full of ash.

Not the black crumble left by her birch. This was finer. Grey as old flour. It lay in a smooth layer over the grate, filling the iron tray beneath. The fire had not made it. She knew the amount of wood she had burnt. She knew the colour of its ash.

Nika put two fingers into it. Cold held there. It stayed on her fingertips after she washed them. It stayed while she packed the tools, while she checked the cameras, while she stood at the door with her coat zipped.

She walked to the lake on the fifth day. The path began behind the cabin and dropped through spruce, then birch, then scrub willow near the shore. Nika had walked it in every season. In summer the roots caught bare toes and lake flies gathered at the wet places. In spring, meltwater ran brown over the stones. In winter, once winter had properly come, the snow softened the slope and made the trees stand farther apart. Now the ground was iron under a thin white skin.

The lake had frozen too early.

Ice stretched from the shore in a smooth black sheet. No snow lay on it. No cracks. No white pressure lines. No ridges where wind had worked at the surface. The far shore was lost in low mist, and the ice ran into it without changing. Nika stopped among the last willows. The lake made no sound.

She looked for the small things that should have been there. Reed stems caught in the edge ice. Bubbles. A stone showing through near the shallows. There was nothing. Only blackness under the surface, held flat.

Something below the ice darkened. Not a body. Not a shadow moving from one place to another. A deepening, circular and slow, opening under the surface where the water should have been too dark to change.

Nika's tongue touched the back of her teeth. Her hands changed before the rest of her did, fingers spreading, then closing against her palms. She stepped back. The willow behind her brushed her coat without a sound. She walked fast to the cabin. Not running. Running would need breath, and her breath had found a small place high in her chest and would not leave it.

The path was longer on the way back. It did not look longer. It gave her the same birches, the same roots, the same split stone with moss on one side. Then it gave them again.

When she reached the clearing, she came out west of the wood store. The cabin door was where she had left it. Closed. The northern camera's green light showed under the eave.

Inside, she packed badly. Socks with tools. Coffee tin open. Thermal camera left on the table, then found and shoved into the bag. Her hands shook around the zip. She watched them do it. Those hands had gutted elk at sixteen. They had carried hot sauna stones with wet cloth and no complaint. They had walked her naked through snow to the lake and back while Elli screamed with laughter behind her. They did not shake.

The zip caught. She pulled once, too hard, and the teeth split open behind the slider. "Fine," she said. She carried the bag under one arm.

Outside, she locked the door. The key turned. The latch set. She pulled the handle once and then again.

From inside came a settling sound. Soft. Deep. The sound a house made at night, except this was not night, and houses settled because heat left them or timber shifted on its nails. The sound ended. The silence after it was easier.

Nika walked to the car without turning around. On the lake road, she drove too fast for the ruts and did not slow until the first house showed yellow through the trees.

At home, the radiator in Nika's bedroom clanked all night. She woke twice to the noise and once to nothing at all. The house was small, timber-framed, built by a man who had measured every room for furniture he already owned. Pipes spoke in the walls. The fridge clicked. A neighbour's dog barked at seven each morning whether there was anything to bark at or not.

On the first day back, she worked a full shift. She cut keys for a woman from the school. She sold a bag of salt, three tins of white paint, and the wrong drill bit to a man who would return it without the receipt. At lunch she ate pea soup in the staff room while Jukka read the sports pages.

At 13:12, she realised she was looking north. Not out of a window. The staff room had no north window. She sat facing a wall calendar from the feed store and knew, without checking, where the cabin was behind it.

She turned her chair. The knowledge turned with her.

At home that evening, she cooked potatoes and fried sausage. The pan smoked because she left it too long before adding fat. She opened a window over the sink, and the cold outside moved in with the smell of wet iron and frozen road. She ate standing up. The plate went into the sink. On the second night, she dreamed nothing and woke tired. On the third, frost marked the inside of her bedroom window.

Nika stood in bare feet on the floorboards and looked at it. The heating had worked all night. The radiator was hot enough that the air above it trembled. The frost began at the lower left corner of the glass, then branched upward in thin white lines.

She touched one line with her nail. It scored under the pressure. "Outside," she said. Her reflection watched from the dark glass.

At work she wrote a list on the back of an invoice and crossed out each item after she had done it. Hinges. Pipe lagging. Salt. Till. Delivery call. None of the words had weight. Her hand kept wanting to write another one. North. She did not write it.

Matti brought the ledger to the bar on Friday in a plastic shopping bag. "Don't spill on it," he said.

Nika looked at his beer. "You first."

He did not smile, but the corner of his mouth moved. He took the book out and set it between them. The cover was black cloth, softened at the corners, with the year 1934 written in white pencil on the front.

"My grandfather kept paths," Matti said. "Before the forestry office took maps from people who knew how to walk."

Nika opened the ledger carefully. The pages smelled of paper kept too long in a dry cupboard. Columns filled most of each spread. Date. Route. Distance. Weather. Notes. The handwriting was small and leaned hard to the right.

At first there was nothing strange in it. Broken marker. Pine down. Bridge plank rotten. Then, in October, numbers in the distance column began to show corrections. Four kilometres crossed out and replaced with six. Six replaced with three and a half. On one page the same northern path was measured three times in one week. None of the numbers matched.

In the margin, in smaller writing: still days. Nika turned the page. Still days. Again.

The final entry for the northern path was dated 17 November. No further.

Nothing after it for that route. The rest of the ledger continued. Southern fence line. Mill road. Parish boundary. Ordinary work, ordinary numbers.

Nika rested her finger beside the last entry without touching the ink. "Did he say why?"

"No."

"Did you ask?"

Matti looked at the bottles behind the bar. "I was young enough to ask once."

"And?"

"He told me to bring in firewood."

Nika closed the ledger. The television showed ski jumping now. A man hung in the air over white ground, arms held tight to his sides. The sound was off.

*

Nika went to the petrol station because the car needed fuel and the station was on the north road. She wanted to know whether turning onto it still felt possible. She filled the tank, went inside, and bought coffee she did not want.

Liisa counted change into Nika's palm. Her nails were painted dark red and chipped at the tips. "You were out by the Aalto cabin," she said.

It had been Elli's grandmother's name. In town, the cabin still belonged to the dead before it belonged to anyone living.

"Camera work."

"Camera won't help."

The coffee machine clicked and began to drip into a paper cup.

"My grandmother used to say if a place wants to be empty, let it." Liisa set a plastic lid beside the coffee. She did not say anything else.

Nika put the lid on. It did not fit properly. Hot liquid touched her thumb.

*

Jukka refused before she had finished asking. "No."

They stood in the stock room among boxes of screws and snow chains. The back door was open to the loading yard. Cold air came in around stacked pallets.

"I need someone to check the cameras with me."

"No."

"I didn't say where."

"You didn't need to."

Nika folded her arms. "You're very helpful."

"I can be helpful here."

"Jukka."

He looked tired suddenly. Older than he had ten minutes ago at the counter, telling a customer which sealant would not crack in frost. He rubbed both hands over his face and left them over his mouth for a moment before dropping them.

"Leave it alone, Nika."

"It's a camera."

"It isn't."

The yard outside was empty. A strip of ice shone along the drain.

"What is it?"

He shook his head.

"Say it."

"No."

"Because you don't know?"

"Because knowing wouldn't help."

Nika waited. The fluorescent light above them hummed, a thin, dirty sound. Jukka picked up a box cutter from the shelf, then set it down again without opening anything.

"Not in November," he said. "Not past the lake road. Not during still days."

That night, Nika sat at her kitchen table with Matti's copied entries, Liisa's words written on the back of a receipt, and Jukka's refusal in her head. She made them into a line because lines were easier to look at than circles.

Still days. Distances wrong. No further. Let it be empty. Leave it alone.

She held the pen over the paper until a bead of ink gathered at the tip and marked the receipt black.

*It wanted me to leave.* She crossed it out.

*It wanted me to come back.* She crossed that out too.

*

On the fourteenth day, she made coffee at 06:30 and let it go cold. The mug was white, not blue. Plain. Bought in a pack of four from the shop, all of them still whole. She held it by the handle until the heat left the ceramic and her fingers had nothing to keep.

At 07:25 she washed it, dried it, and put it in the wrong cupboard.

She opened the shop with Jukka. The morning customers came in stamping snow grit from their boots. Nika cut rope, counted change, answered questions. Her hands did the work. They were good hands and knew the small weights of things. A packet of screws. A roll of tape. A key blank before the blade touched it.

At 11:42 she looked at an invoice and saw that she had written the same product code in every box. Jukka took it from her. "Go," he said.

She met his eyes. "Go where?"

"Don't make me answer that."

Outside, the sky was white from edge to edge. She drove without the radio. The road was empty. Past the lake, mist sat between the trees in low strips. The last kilometre took sound out of the car piece by piece. First the heater fan. Then the vibration from the tyres. Then the small rattle in the passenger door that had been there for three years.

The cabin appeared. Nika parked and left both hands on the wheel. Five minutes passed by the dashboard clock. She watched each minute change. 12:31. 12:32. 12:33. 12:34. 12:35.

The cabin door was ajar.

Not open. A gap of ten centimetres, black from top to bottom. She had locked it. She knew the feel of the latch setting, the second pull on the handle, the key turned back and put under the north log because Elli still believed in hiding places from childhood.

At 12:36, Nika got out of the car. The cold was sharp and ordinary. It bit her cheeks, cleared her nose, put water in her eyes. Her boots broke the crust on the snow. Each step sounded exactly as it should. The door did not move as she approached.

She pushed it open with two fingers. Warmth came out. Not the narrow heat around a new fire. Whole-room warmth. Lived-in warmth. It carried smoke, dry wool, coffee, and the faint sweetness of split birch stacked too close to the hearth.

The fire was burning low and steady. Not freshly lit. The logs had collapsed inward and glowed along their centres. Someone had fed it hours ago, then stayed near enough to keep it even.

The chairs faced the hearth in a half-circle. Nika stayed at the threshold.

The book stood on the shelf, spine-out and flush with the others. The table was clear. The floor had been swept. The kettle sat on the stove. Elli's blue mug should have been there with its chipped rim, but of course it was not. She had carried its pieces home in a paper bag and put them in the outside bin and stood there in the dark after the lid closed.

Everything was exactly right. The warmth moved over her boots. Her shoulders lowered before she told them to. The knot under her breastbone loosened by one turn, then another. She was tired. The tiredness had been there for days, thick through her arms, behind her eyes, in the hinge of her jaw. The cabin held heat. The road home would still be there later. The chair nearest the hearth stood turned out from the others by the width of a hand.

Elli at twelve, head sideways in that chair, asleep after the lake. Nika covering her feet without waking her.

*Sit down. Rest.*

She took one step inside.

The floorboard under her boot gave its familiar creak. *You're home.* Her hand closed on the door frame.

The old part of her, the part that checked ice before stepping and touched axe heads away from the blade and knew when meat had turned without bringing it to her nose, reached the warmth and knew it.

The fire should not have been burning. No one had lit it. No one had fed it. No one had swept the floor. No one had set the chairs to make a place for her and left the place open. Everything was exactly right.

Nika said, "No." The fire dimmed.

Warmth withdrew from her boots, her knees, her hands on the frame. The room beneath it came through. Cold, wide, patient. The chairs remained where they were. Nika stepped back.

Outside, air struck her face with clean force. The silence returned with its hard edge. It sat among the trees, in the snow crust, under the eave where the northern camera watched with its steady green light.

She pulled the door shut. The latch resisted once, then set. She locked it. The key shook against the ring, a small bright sound. She put it under the north log and walked to the car without looking back. The engine started on the second try. She drove home.

*

At midnight, Nika sat at the kitchen table with cold coffee and her phone in front of her. The house had gone quiet around the useful sounds. Radiator. Fridge. The settling of timber. Her own breathing when she noticed it and then could not stop noticing. The receipt with her crossed-out sentences lay beside the cup, folded twice. She unfolded it and turned it over. Liisa's words sat on the back where she had written them. *If a place wants to be empty, let it.* The cabin was not hers to let.

She called Elli. The line rang long enough for Nika to see, in the dark window over the sink, her own face lit by the phone. One cheekbone. One eye. The white edge of the table. Voicemail picked up. Nika closed her eyes. She had never asked Elli to come back. The asking ran the other way.

"Elli. It's me. You need to come home."

The words left her and made space for the next ones. "Something's wrong with the cabin. I don't know what." The kitchen window ticked softly.

"Don't go up on your own. Wait."

"It's..." Nika opened her eyes. Frost had spread from the lower left corner of the glass while she sat there. Thin branches, pale against black.

"It's lying out there," she said.

She ended the call. The phone screen went dark. Nika sat with her hand still around it.

Outside, snow fell through motionless air. Straight down past the kitchen window. It thickened the yard, the road, the stacked wood by the shed.

She could not see the cabin from here. The kitchen wall stayed where it was. North stayed behind it.
